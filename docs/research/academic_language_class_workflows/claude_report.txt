# How US language classes actually work, from bell to bell

**The daily reality of teaching ESL, Spanish, or Mandarin in American schools revolves around thematic textbook chapters, conjugation-heavy worksheets, proficiency rubrics adapted from ACTFL guidelines, and a grading workload that pushes teachers to spend $895/year out of pocket on materials and 5+ hours weekly on assessment.** Across middle school, high school, and introductory college, the structure is remarkably consistent: thematic-grammar hybrid units, 4–6 chapters per semester, and a growing but incomplete shift from traditional grammar drills toward proficiency-based instruction aligned with ACTFL Can-Do Statements. The pain points are equally consistent — grading 150 students' speaking assessments remains the single biggest bottleneck, heritage speakers sit bored in beginner classes, and 64% of the supplemental materials teachers buy on Teachers Pay Teachers are rated "not worth using" by researchers. This report maps every dimension of how these classes function in practice, drawing on real syllabi, district curriculum guides, TPT listings, AP scoring guidelines, teacher blogs, and survey data.

---

## Semesters run on thematic-grammar chapters, 4–6 per term

The dominant organizational pattern across all three languages and all levels is the **thematic-grammar hybrid unit**: each chapter centers on a life theme (family, school, food, travel) while embedding 2–4 grammar points within that theme. This structure is universal across the major textbooks — Avancemos, Descubre, Realidades, Panorama, and Integrated Chinese.

A real Avancemos 1 pacing guide from a South Carolina district maps the full year: **8 units plus a novel**, beginning with a 5-week preliminary chapter on greetings and the alphabet, then progressing through subject pronouns and *gustar* (Q1), -AR verbs and *estar* (Q2), -ER/-IR verbs and stem-changing verbs (Q3), and ending with commands and the novel *Pobre Ana* (Q4). Each unit gets 2–4 weeks, with 2 quizzes and 1 unit test per unit, plus quarterly performance assessments. York College/CUNY's SPAN 101 syllabus covers Lecciones 1–5 of Panorama (Vista Higher Learning) across 15 weeks — roughly 2–3 weeks per chapter, with five chapter tests and a final exam.

**The textbook landscape is shifting fast.** HMH announced Avancemos is retired and no longer available for purchase, though current adoptions remain supported. Vista Higher Learning (Descubre, Portales, Senderos) is gaining significant market share, with its Supersite digital platform now standard at hundreds of colleges. A growing movement rejects textbooks entirely: Allison Wienhold of Mis Clases Locas teaches Spanish 1–4 using novels, films, and music through comprehensible input (CI) methods, and Martina Bex's SOMOS curriculum — sold through TPT — has become a de facto alternative textbook for thousands of teachers.

For Mandarin, **Integrated Chinese (Cheng & Tsui, 4th edition) dominates** at both the high school and college levels. The University of Minnesota's College in the Schools program uses it directly for dual-enrollment Chinese, and Middlebury's Language Schools use it at upper levels. The standard college sequence covers 10 lessons per semester across 4 semesters (2 volumes × 2 levels), introducing roughly 350 vocabulary items per level. High school Mandarin programs like IMSA's organize thematically around the student's world — "I am…" → "My family is…" → "My daily routine" — with instruction predominantly in Chinese and no textbook, using teacher-created materials posted on Schoology.

ESL is **fundamentally different** in its organizing logic. Curricula follow WIDA English Language Development Standards (used by 40+ states), organized by proficiency levels (Entering through Reaching) and academic language functions (Narrate, Inform, Explain, Argue) rather than thematic vocabulary chapters. Rutherford Public Schools (NJ) structures its K–12 ESL curriculum around the four language domains — Listening, Speaking, Reading, Writing — mapped to WIDA proficiency levels 1–6, with students grouped heterogeneously by school location, not proficiency. No single ESL textbook dominates; districts typically combine published materials (National Geographic Reach Higher, Vista's Get Ready!) with teacher-created resources, and newcomer programs operate on a separate accelerated track for the first 6–9 weeks.

Class scheduling varies significantly. Traditional schedules run **45–55 minutes daily**, while block schedules run **80–99 minutes every other day**. AnneMarie Chase describes her school's A/B block at 90 minutes, and Allison Wienhold teaches on a 99-minute block, planning 7–8 activities per period to maintain engagement. College intro courses typically meet MWF for 50 minutes or TR for 75 minutes, with intensive programs (like UIUC's 5-credit Chinese 201) meeting daily.

## Conjugation drills and mystery pictures define the worksheet stack

The actual assignments students receive fall into predictable patterns that shift by level. At the middle school and Spanish 1 level, **fill-in-the-blank conjugation, vocabulary matching, and conjugation tables dominate**. A top-selling TPT product offers "6 hand-drawn worksheets" where students provide verb endings, complete word searches, translate, fill a 6×6 verb Sudoku, and write sentences — covering regular -AR, -ER, -IR, Ser, Estar, and Tener. Gamified conjugation drills are enormously popular: "Color by Code" mystery picture games, where correct conjugations reveal hidden images in self-checking Google Sheets, are consistent best-sellers.

At Spanish 2–3, reading comprehension passages become more prominent, particularly through Martina Bex's "El Mundo en Tus Manos" — a biweekly Spanish news publication offering **100 breaking news stories, 300 articles, 200 worksheets, and dozens of activities**, with versions differentiated from Level 1 through AP. Dialogue writing, sentence translation, and paragraph writing emerge. Burke County High School (GA) assigns skits with rubrics covering content, pronunciation, and fluency.

At the AP and college level, the shift is dramatic: source-based argumentative essays, email replies, simulated conversations, and cultural comparison presentations mirror the AP Spanish exam format. The AP exam itself runs **3 hours, 3 minutes** — 65 multiple-choice questions across print and audio sources (50% of score) plus 4 free-response tasks: an email reply (15 min), an argumentative essay synthesizing 3 sources including audio (55 min), a simulated conversation with 5 exchanges of 20 seconds each, and a 2-minute cultural comparison presentation.

Mandarin worksheets have a unique component: **character writing practice with stroke order diagrams** dominates at all levels. Arch Chinese generates custom worksheets with stroke sequences, radicals, pinyin, and English definitions across 45+ worksheet tools. The Ling-Ling Chinese site provides college-level worksheets aligned to Integrated Chinese, including vocabulary quizzes requiring both character recognition and pinyin, oral performance sheets ("Invite a Friend to a Movie"), and sentence unscrambling activities.

For ESL, worksheets emphasize **visual supports, sentence frames in both L1 and English, and content-area vocabulary** (science, social studies, math terms). WIDA's framework means ESL materials are differentiated by proficiency level within a single classroom — a Level 1 student labels pictures while a Level 4 student writes explanatory paragraphs on the same topic.

Standardized assessments exert enormous influence on classroom practice. Beyond the AP exam, the **WIDA ACCESS for ELLs** tests Listening, Reading, Speaking, and Writing annually for all K–12 English learners in 41 states, using computer-adaptive routing. The **STAMP 4S** (Avant Assessment) assesses proficiency in 15 languages across 4 skills, with scores mapped directly to ACTFL levels, and is increasingly used for Seal of Biliteracy qualification. The **ACTFL OPI** remains the gold standard for speaking proficiency — a live 15–30 minute telephone conversation with a certified tester, rating from Novice Low through Superior.

## ACTFL proficiency rubrics are the backbone, but implementation varies wildly

The dominant assessment framework for world languages is the **ACTFL Proficiency Guidelines (updated 2024)**, which use the FACT criteria: Functions and tasks, Accuracy, Context and content, and Text type. Five major levels (Novice → Intermediate → Advanced → Superior → Distinguished), with the first three subdivided into Low/Mid/High, yield 11 levels total. For ESL, the parallel framework is **WIDA's 6-level scale** (Entering → Emerging → Developing → Expanding → Bridging → Reaching), which differs from ACTFL in its focus on academic language across content areas rather than broader communicative proficiency.

The AP Spanish rubrics from College Board represent the most standardized high-stakes implementation: **5-point holistic scales** for all four tasks, assessing task completion, comprehensibility, vocabulary range, grammar accuracy, register appropriateness, and sentence complexity simultaneously. A score of 5 on the interpersonal speaking rubric requires "fully understandable, with ease and clarity" and "varied and appropriate vocabulary and idiomatic language."

Ohio's Department of Education provides the most widely referenced free rubric collection for classroom use, including separate rubrics for presentational speaking, presentational writing, interpersonal communication (both performance and proficiency versions), and a gradebook conversion chart mapping proficiency levels to numeric scores. Their Novice Interpersonal rubric evaluates Pronunciation, Fluency, Quality of Communication, Vocabulary, and Language Control — with the important note that "Novice learners may sound surprisingly fluent when using memorized language," so the rubric distinguishes memorized phrases from created speech.

The movement toward **proficiency-based grading** is significant but far from universal. Teachers adopting this approach restructure gradebook categories from traditional (tests 40%, quizzes 20%, homework 20%, participation 20%) to skill-based (**Listening 20%, Speaking 20%, Reading 20%, Writing 20%, Miscellaneous 20%**). Wayside Publishing describes the grading logic: A = all summatives at Meets/Exceeds; B = one Partial; C = two Partial; D = one Lacks; F = two or more Lacks. Homework is removed as a graded category; vocabulary and grammar fold into skill rubrics. Madame Shepard converts Ohio's proficiency rubrics to a 10-point scale: Novice Mid = 7/10, Novice High = 8/10, Intermediate Low = 9/10, Intermediate Mid = 10/10.

A critical distinction teachers make: **performance rubrics** (used for end-of-unit assessments on practiced material) differ from **proficiency rubrics** (used 2–3 times per year on unfamiliar topics measuring spontaneous ability). As one Ohio teacher notes, "Teachers can't grade proficiency. There are specialized assessments for it. We hope for proficiency, but we measure performance."

The **Integrated Performance Assessment (IPA)**, ACTFL's recommended format, connects three tasks through a central theme: an interpretive task (reading/listening comprehension scored for accuracy), an interpersonal task (conversation scored by rubric), and a presentational task (writing/speaking scored by rubric). The IPA Manual, now in its 3rd edition (2023), provides templates and rubrics for K–16 implementation.

## Google Classroom plus clipboard: how the assign-collect-grade cycle works

**Google Classroom dominates K–12 assignment distribution**, with its user base quadrupling from 40 million to 150 million during the pandemic. In higher education, Canvas holds **39% of the North American LMS market**, followed by Blackboard at 19%. The K–12 LMS landscape is more fragmented: Canvas at ~28%, Google Classroom at ~24%, Schoology at ~22%. By 2022, 68% of teachers were using an online LMS to collect and return student work.

Language teachers layer specialized tools on top of their LMS. **Conjuguemos** — used by 32,000+ schools and 4.2 million students — auto-grades verb conjugation, vocabulary, and grammar drills, emailing teachers results upon completion. Vista Higher Learning's Supersite, the primary digital platform for college language courses, offers 800,000+ auto-graded activities with LMS grade passback to Canvas, Blackboard, or Moodle. EdPuzzle embeds comprehension questions into videos for auto-graded listening practice.

Speaking assessment collection splits between two approaches. For **in-class live observation**, teachers circulate with clipboards and mini-rubrics during paired conversations — one teacher describes having "two students at my desk, two on deck" while the rest work independently. For **asynchronous recording**, Flipgrid (now Microsoft Flip) became the standard: teachers create a topic, students record 15-second to 5-minute videos, and teachers review and score at their convenience. A Spanish teacher describes the workflow: "All you have to do is choose a grade of 1–5 and type a few comments. Not only does every student get to speak, every student gets feedback."

Teachers spend a **median of 5 hours per week grading**, according to the 2022 EdWeek/Merrimack College survey, out of a median 54-hour work week. A 2024 RAND survey found 88% of teachers work 41–80+ hours per week despite contracts requiring only 21–40. Auto-graded tools (Conjuguemos, Google Forms quizzes, Canvas quizzes) provide instant feedback on discrete-point exercises. Writing and speaking assessments take **1–2 weeks for turnaround** based on teacher reports, with proficiency-based teachers emphasizing ongoing formative feedback rather than summative grade returns.

For progress tracking, **PowerSchool** dominates K–12 student information systems at 23% market share (serving 60+ million students worldwide), followed by Infinite Campus (~10%) and Skyward (~7%). The LMS gradebook typically serves as the teacher's working gradebook, with final grades syncing to the district SIS. No widely adopted classroom-level software exists for ongoing, mode-specific proficiency tracking — teachers who track ACTFL-aligned proficiency progression do so manually, often in Google Sheets workbooks with tabs for each class, translating proficiency levels into the percentage grades their district gradebooks require.

## Speaking assessment is the bottleneck; heritage speakers are the blind spot

The research reveals a consistent hierarchy of pain points, with **oral assessment grading as the undisputed #1 frustration** across all language types and levels.

With a typical load of **150 students across 5 sections**, spending even 5 minutes per speaking rubric becomes mathematically unfeasible — that's 12.5 hours of grading for a single round of assessments. Teachers describe years of frustration before finding workable systems: "World language speaking assessments frustrated me for many years until I figured out how to incorporate them into my lessons in a time-efficient manner." Current workarounds include assessing only during class time with simple 4-point rubrics, grading half the class live and half via recordings, and using random pairing at the teacher's desk while other students work independently. Even with these strategies, the subjectivity inherent in speaking assessment — "there is an inherent level of subjectivity and bias" — makes standardized evaluation nearly impossible at scale.

**Heritage speakers in beginner classes** represent a structural problem that teacher training programs largely ignore. When heritage Spanish speakers — students who grew up hearing or speaking Spanish at home — land in Spanish 1, "boredom sets in quickly for any mind that is unchallenged on a daily basis," and teachers find it "nearly impossible to regularly meet the needs of beginning language learners while creating meaningful activities for students who already comprehend Spanish at a high level." Heritage Spanish teaching is described as "a way bigger job than anything I was ever prepared for during my studies at University. It's simply not taught when you go to school to become a language teacher." The problem extends to identity: "Far too many heritage speakers are victims of toxic sentiments and feel as this student did: 'I always thought my Spanish was bad because I didn't follow the rules of my high school textbook.'"

Multi-level ESL classrooms present an analogous challenge. Teachers describe the reality bluntly: "You're teaching multiple proficiency levels — sometimes in the same 45-minute block. There's often no curriculum. You're expected to support language and content at the same time. You're probably still writing your own lessons from scratch." Community-based programs face the most extreme version, with students varying not just in English proficiency but in first-language literacy and formal education experience.

Material creation consumes enormous teacher time and money. **80%+ of US teachers use Teachers Pay Teachers**, with almost three-quarters spending exclusively their own money on materials. A 2025 AdoptAClassroom survey found teachers spent an average of **$895 out of pocket** during 2024–25, up 49% since 2015, with 97% saying their school-provided budget (median $200) was insufficient. Yet a USC study found **64% of TPT materials "should not be used" or were "probably not worth using,"** scoring 0 or 1 on a 0–3 quality scale and frequently lacking differentiation guidance or standards alignment.

Homework completion has collapsed to the point where many language teachers have simply stopped assigning it. Multiple teachers report that proficiency-based grading accelerated this: "Since this change, the amount of times I give homework has gone down drastically due to the fact that so many fewer students do it" when it doesn't affect grades. Student reluctance to speak in the target language compounds engagement challenges — one teacher describes the transformation coming only "when I let go of 'getting my students to speak up.'"

The systemic backdrop makes all of this harder. ACTFL recommends a maximum of **15 students per language class**, but actual class sizes routinely exceed 30. As many as **34% of world language teachers cite large classes as a reason for leaving the profession**. At least **49 of 56 US states and territories experienced world language teacher shortages** in 2017–18, and 74% of districts had trouble filling positions for 2024–25. Foreign language positions rank among the hardest to fill nationwide, alongside special education and science.

## Where software could transform the workflow

The research points to five high-impact gaps where technology — particularly AI — could meaningfully reduce teacher burden without replacing human judgment:

- **Automated first-pass scoring of recorded speech**: The 12.5-hour speaking assessment bottleneck is the clearest opportunity. AI could provide initial proficiency-level estimates and flag students needing human review, cutting grading time by 60–80%. Current AI speech scoring research shows "high agreement with human scores" but warns systems "often prioritize surface-level textual features over deeper communicative competence."
- **Writing feedback at scale**: Generating grammar, vocabulary, and comprehensibility feedback on student paragraphs in the target language — not replacing teacher assessment, but providing the detailed line-level annotation teachers cannot deliver for 150 students.
- **Longitudinal proficiency dashboards**: No widely adopted tool tracks individual students' ACTFL-aligned proficiency growth across all four modes over time. Teachers currently jerry-rig Google Sheets or lose nuance by converting proficiency levels to percentages for district gradebooks.
- **Differentiated material generation**: Automatically adapting a single reading passage or activity to multiple proficiency levels — the core challenge in multi-level ESL and heritage-speaker classrooms — would address the problem one teacher described as "creating 5 versions of everything."
- **AI conversation partners for practice**: Giving students low-stakes speaking practice before high-stakes assessments could address both the engagement challenge ("students who refuse to speak") and the assessment bottleneck by ensuring students arrive better prepared.

Teacher attitudes toward AI remain mixed but increasingly positive: **61% of teachers used AI in some capacity by January 2026** (up from 34% in 2023), and those using it weekly estimated saving ~5.9 hours per week. However, usage concentrates on planning and material creation rather than direct grading, reflecting ongoing concerns about accuracy, fairness, and construct validity. The US Department of Education's 2023 AI report warns that "AI may provide information that appears authentic, but actually is inaccurate," emphasizing that humans must remain in the loop — a constraint that aligns with how language teachers think about assessment: rubrics measure communicative competence, not just surface accuracy, and that judgment remains irreducibly human.

---

## Conclusion

US language classes operate on a remarkably stable infrastructure — thematic textbook chapters, conjugation worksheets, ACTFL-aligned rubrics, Google Classroom distribution — while straining against class sizes double the recommended maximum and a teacher workforce spending nearly $900 per year of personal money on materials, much of it low quality. The most consequential divide in the field is not between languages or levels but between traditional grammar-drill instruction and the proficiency-based/comprehensible input movement, which reorganizes grading around communicative modes, replaces textbooks with novels and authentic media, and measures what students can do rather than what they know about. The biggest unrealized opportunity sits at the intersection of the speaking assessment bottleneck and AI capabilities: teachers grading 150 oral assessments by hand remains the workflow most ripe for technological transformation, provided systems can move beyond surface-level feature detection to genuinely assess communicative competence.
