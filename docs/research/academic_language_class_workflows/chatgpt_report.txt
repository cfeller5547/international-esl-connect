Executive Summary
Academic language courses are highly structured: A typical HS Spanish 1 curriculum covers basic grammar (present tense, “to be/like,” past tense) and conversational topics (self-intros, school, family, food, directions)
. Mandarin 1 focuses on pinyin/tones, basic characters, greetings, numbers and simple daily routines
. ESL classes focus on all four domains (listening, speaking, reading, writing), aligned to WIDA/Common Core, with writing-heavy projects and vocabulary-building (e.g. autobiographies, essays)
. Sample syllabi (see table) show each course uses thematic units and frequent low-stakes practice.

Assignments are paper/text driven: Common tasks include vocabulary drills (lists, flashcards/Quizlet), conjugation/grammar worksheets, reading and listening comprehension exercises, speaking activities (dialogues, presentations), writing tasks (journals, letters, essays), and cultural projects. For example, a Mandarin program uses “character writing quizzes” each lesson
, paired conversation drills, and multi-page cultural project write-ups
. A Spanish class uses worksheets, group projects and written prompts
. In all subjects, unit quizzes and tests (written and oral) are routine (see Rubrics).

Grading rubrics are explicit: Language educators typically use detailed rubrics for speaking and writing. New York State’s world-language rubrics (Checkpoint C) score writing on criteria like task completion, organization, syntax and vocabulary
. A speaking rubric rates pronunciation, fluency, syntax, vocabulary on a 0–5 scale
. In practice, students know work is evaluated by accuracy and use of target language. Teachers often share these rubrics with students at unit start to set expectations
.

Teacher workflow is manual and time-intensive: Interviews and resources indicate instructors typically handle placement, lesson design, homework creation, grading and tracking by hand. For example, one HS teacher’s workflow: placement test → unit-based lesson plans → print or upload assignments → collect submissions (paper or LMS) → grade via rubric → record scores. Major time sinks are lesson planning, creating worksheets/quizzes, and grading. A recent survey finds US teachers spend ~9.9 hours/week grading alone
, with 62% citing grading as “one of the worst aspects” of teaching
. (See workflow diagram below.)

Existing tools partly address needs but gaps remain: Many teachers use Google Workspace/LMS (Classroom/Canvas) to distribute assignments, Quizlet/Kahoot for practice, and traditional textbooks. However, these are often generic (vocabulary drills, games) rather than curriculum-aligned. For instance, a language educator notes that Google Forms can auto-grade quizzes, eliminating “hours of manual grading”
, and Quizlet’s audio flashcards give valuable pronunciation practice
. Yet no tool seamlessly integrates daily practice with the exact vocabulary/grammar of a student’s specific class.

Pain points ripe for software solutions: Top pain points include: grading workload (10+ hours/week)
; lesson prep time; and disconnect between class and out-of-class practice. Many students lack structured practice outside class (aside from generic apps). Teachers want automated grading/feedback and easily reusable, curriculum-specific exercises. Also, in-class challenges like students “speaking too much English” are noted
. A specialized app could automate homework feedback, align exercises to exact class topics, and provide guided daily speaking practice, directly attacking these pain points.

Open questions for validation: How do teachers currently assess placement and progress (tests, portfolios)? What specific tasks take the most time (lesson prep vs grading vs feedback)? How do students practice outside class, and how motivated are they by current tools? What features would prompt teachers and parents to subscribe? Quick interviews (see end) with 5–10 teachers and students can confirm these points and surface additional automation ideas.

1. Course Structures by Level and Language
Language courses are typically organized into year-long sequences with 4–5 units per year, each focusing on specific themes and grammar. Below are representative outlines:

Spanish (e.g. HS Spanish 1–3): A sample Spanish 1 syllabus describes units on introductions, school life, family, etc., covering present tense verbs, subject-verb agreement, “ser/estar,” * gustar, and an introduction to past tense
. Materials often include a standard textbook (e.g. Avancemos). Progression is generally: Year 1 – basic conversation and present tenses; Year 2 – past tenses and expanded themes (travel, dining, sports); Year 3+ – future tenses, subjunctive mood, authentic readings. Teachers align to state/world-language standards (e.g. ACTFL).

Mandarin Chinese (e.g. HS Chinese 1–2): Mandarin 1 covers pinyin and tones, basic strokes, greetings, family, numbers, dates, and simple Q&A. For example, one curriculum’s Unit 1 (Greetings) focuses on pinyin, tones, writing strokes, and introducing names/dates
. Cultural topics (Chinese festivals, social customs) are interwoven
. By Chinese 2, students learn family/age, school, hobbies, and more verb forms. Assessments include character-writing quizzes (with new vocab and pinyin) and oral dialogs
.

ESL (English as a Second Language): ESL classes span multiple levels (e.g. Beginner through Advanced) rather than grade-level. A high-school ESL I class emphasizes academic English: reading, writing, speaking, listening for classroom success. For instance, one syllabus notes a “rigorous class” aligned with WIDA standards and Common Core, with units on personal background, summarizing texts, persuasive writing, research reports, etc.
. Typical units involve writing autobiographies, essays, projects (detective case reports, ethical debates), with vocabulary and grammar integrated. ESL II/III continue advancing complexity, often with more literature or subject-area content.

2. Common Assignment Types
Teachers use a mix of traditional worksheets and project-based tasks. Common assignment categories (with examples) include:

Vocabulary Practice: Flashcards/Word lists: Students memorize thematic word lists. Many teachers use Quizlet flashcard sets or matching exercises for review
. (E.g. creating a Quizlet set for “family” vocabulary with audio pronunciation
.)
Grammar Drills/Conjugation: Worksheets filling in verb tables or sentences. Spanish classes often use blank-conjugation grids or fill-in exercises for regular/irregular verbs. Chinese classes have character writing quizzes: e.g., “each lesson has 2 word quizzes” including new characters, pinyin and meanings
.
Reading Comprehension: Short passages + questions. Students read a paragraph on a cultural topic or story in target language, then answer questions. (Often sourced from textbooks or adapted articles.)
Listening Comprehension: Audio or video tasks. Students listen to dialogues or announcements, then do true/false or answer questions. (Sometimes worksheets accompany teacher-read audio or online clips.)
Speaking Activities: Dialogues and presentations. In class, students practice scripted dialogues in pairs or give short presentations (e.g. “introduce your family” or “describe your weekend”). Teachers may assign recorded speaking homework (e.g. record a spoken paragraph on Seesaw).
Writing Tasks: Journals, letters, essays. Journal prompts (daily or weekly reflections) and letters (e.g. write to a pen pal) are common. Longer essays or creative stories appear at higher levels. An ESL unit, for instance, might assign a multi-paragraph persuasive essay or book report
.
Cultural Projects: Research or creative projects. E.g., “China Moon Festival project: after a video, students write a 2–3 page guided report”
. Spanish classes might have cooking labs, posters about festivals, or group skits.
Quizzes & Tests: Frequent checks: short quizzes on vocabulary/grammar (~5–10 questions), unit tests (written sections and sometimes oral interviews). For example, Mandarin classes use end-of-unit written tests plus an oral “Ask & Answer” format speaking exam
. Spanish/ESL classes typically have both.
Games/Interactive: In-class review games. Teachers often reinforce material with Kahoot, Quizizz, or in-class matching games. These are less formal assignments but part of practice. (A world-language educator recommends Kahoot for engagement and form- versus summative use
.)

3. Quizzes, Exams, and Grading Rubrics
Formative quizzes and summative tests are core. Teachers commonly give short quizzes at end of each unit (vocab/grammar) and midterm/final exams. Many assessments include a speaking component. For instance, one ESL class has “midterm” and “final” exams each semester
, while the Mandarin 1 curriculum includes both written exams and an oral “Ask & Answer” speaking test
.

Grading rubrics: To ensure consistency, teachers often use rubrics. New York’s World Languages rubrics (Checkpoint C) exemplify this approach. A speaking rubric may score Pronunciation, Fluency, Syntax, and Vocabulary each 0–5
. A writing rubric typically rates Task Fulfillment, Organization, Vocabulary, and Syntax on 4–5 point scales
. For example, the writing rubric’s top score (5) requires “well-organized and articulated” writing with correct grammar and creative vocabulary use
. Lower scores note errors or limited language. Teachers often share these criteria with students so they know expectations beforehand
.

Such rubrics mean students’ grades hinge on accuracy and complexity in L2 output, not just completion. For example, a student’s Spanish essay must demonstrate subject-verb agreement and use of new structures to earn top marks
. Similarly, speaking tests assess correct pronunciation of new sounds/tones. In practice, each major assignment or exam is graded by these standards, often using checklists or point scales that align with report-card grades.

Key points:

Onboarding/Placement: In practice, new students may take a placement test (often from district or state ELL/LOTE standards) to identify their proficiency. The teacher enrolls them in the appropriate level/class. (E.g. WIDA ACCESS for ESL, or a district Spanish placement quiz.) If no test, teachers may give sample exercises on first day to gauge level.
Lesson Planning: Teachers review pacing guides/curriculum maps (often district-provided) and plan each unit’s lessons. This involves selecting topics from the standard (e.g. textbooks like Avancemos or Mandarin textbooks). Planning includes writing lesson objectives, collecting or creating materials (presentations, handouts, slides), and sometimes collaborating with other teachers. This step (B) is a major time sink.
Assignment Creation: The teacher creates or curates homework and practice assignments (worksheets, quizzes, projects). Often this means customizing material to current unit vocabulary/grammar, or copying text from textbooks into printable form. Tools like Google Docs or Quizlet sets are used here. This step (C) is also very time-consuming, as found in teacher reports.
Distribution: Assignments are distributed to students. This may be by handing out printed worksheets, posting to Google Classroom/Canvas, or emailing links. Many teachers mention using Google Drive/LMS to share materials (syllabus, worksheets, slide decks)
.
Students Complete Work: Students work on assignments either in class or as homework.
Collection: Completed work is collected. In modern classes this often involves Google Classroom: students submit digital files (Docs, PDFs, photos of handwriting) or click answers in Forms/Quizizz. Otherwise, papers are collected by hand.
Grading & Feedback: Manual grading is typically required for writing and speaking tasks. Quizzes may auto-grade if done on Google Forms or Quizlet. Teachers score each assignment according to the rubric and write feedback. According to a US survey, teachers average 9.9 hours/week grading
, confirming that grading (step G) dominates time. The Learnosity survey found 62% of teachers say grading is one of the worst parts of their job
.
Progress Recording: Grades/scores are entered into a gradebook (digital or paper). Many schools use platforms (PowerSchool, Infinite Campus) tied to the LMS to record progress.
Communication: Teachers may communicate results to students/parents via online portals, emails, or printed reports. (At minimum, students are expected to check an online homework board if absent
.)
Assessments/Exams: Periodically, the teacher administers unit tests or projects (both formative and summative). These are planned in advance of a unit’s end, and cover all four skills as noted earlier.
In this cycle, lesson planning, assignment creation, and grading stand out as recurring bottlenecks. Software that automates quiz generation, auto-grading, or provides pre-made curriculum-aligned exercises could significantly reduce these burdens.

5. Tools Currently Used & Friction
World-language teachers leverage common edtech but face mismatches:

LMS/Platform: Google Classroom dominates US K-12 classrooms. As of 2022, ~60,000 U.S. schools used Google Classroom
. It is used to post assignments, collect work, and communicate. Canvas (Instructure) has far fewer K-12 customers (∼8,282 organizations worldwide, ~2.7% market share)
. Many districts also use Schoology, Teams, etc., but teachers often default to Google. Friction: these platforms are generic (not language-specific) and require manual setup each term.

Content Creation Tools: Teachers commonly use Google Docs/Drive for creating and storing worksheets
. Presentations (Slides/PowerPoint) for class. Quizlet is widely used for vocab; a teacher praises its audio flashcards as “critical” for vocabulary acquisition
. Quizizz/Kahoot/Gimkit are popular for quiz games. Friction: Content still must be authored for each unit, and sharing assignments across classes can be ad-hoc.

Textbooks & Print Resources: Many classes use traditional textbooks (e.g. Avancemos for Spanish, Cheng & Tsui books for Chinese, Edge series for ESL) which come with workbooks. Teachers may photocopy textbook exercises or buy materials from TPT sites. Friction: Static curriculum with limited updates; not interactive.

Professional Resources: District-provided pacing guides and state standards (ACTFL, CEFR, WIDA) inform curriculum. Teachers rarely find a one-stop solution; instead they patch together.

In summary, current tools partially address needs but leave gaps. For example, a teacher points out Google Forms “automatically grade” quizzes
, saving manual effort. However, no mainstream app tailors itself to the exact lesson plan content that a teacher uses. A teacher must still align any digital content with the scope of their class. Communication tools (email, LMS announcements) handle notifications, but there is no integrated “homework help” or daily practice aligned to school units.

Implications: A SaaS that automates grading/feedback (especially for writing/speaking), provides curriculum-aligned practice, and integrates seamlessly with teachers’ existing workflow could address several top pain points at once. For example, if quizzes auto-grade and update the gradebook, and if practice questions are drawn from the class’s current vocabulary/grammar, students get relevant daily work while teachers save hours.

7. Next Steps: Validation & Interviews
Based on the above, key open questions remain. We recommend interviewing 5–10 teachers and students to validate assumptions. Possible questions:

For Teachers:

“What takes up most of your time each week? (e.g. planning lessons, creating assignments, grading?)” – To confirm top time sinks.
“How do you currently create or find practice exercises for your daily units?” – To see if they struggle to find aligned content.
“What tools do you use for homework and quizzes? What are their strengths/limitations?” – To assess tool gap and discover new ones.
“How do you assess and track student progress in the four language skills?” – To map current assessment workflow.
“What features would make you excited to use a new language-learning tool?” – To pinpoint must-have functions.
“How do you communicate assignments and feedback to students/parents?” – To see how integrated solution needs to be.
“Have you used AI or automated tools for language teaching? What was your experience?” – To gauge openness to AI features.
For Students:

“How do you practice the language outside of class?” – Understand use of apps or tech.
“What do you like or dislike about apps you use (Duolingo, Quizlet, etc.)?” – Identify unmet needs.
“What frustrates you about doing homework or studying languages?” – Uncover pain (e.g. boring, too generic).
“Would you use an app if it gave instant feedback on your school assignments?” – Test interest in targeted tool.
“What motivates you to keep learning this language?” – Inform retention strategies.
